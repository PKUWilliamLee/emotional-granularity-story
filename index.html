      05 Books over time

      Historical signal

      The book data does not tell a simple collapse story.

      Across NRC emotion categories, diversity dips in the mid-century and partially recovers later. Some emotion families, like joy, lose diversity; others, like surprise and anticipation, become more spread out.

      So "we've lost the words for feelings" isn't quite right. It's closer to: we've traded some words for others. That sent us looking somewhere less polished than novels — everyday speech.

      06 Reddit comments

      Everyday expression

      Online emotion is often present, but not always named.

      Out of 38,000+ emotional Reddit comments, only about 3 in 10 use an actual emotion word. That doesn't mean the rest feel nothing — it means feeling leaks out through ALL CAPS, a well-placed swear, or just telling the story and letting you feel it too.

      And when people do reach for a word, they grab the closest one, not the best one: basic, everyday labels make up 94% of what gets used. The precise, specific words? Barely half a percent.

      07 AI vs Human

      Movie review experiment

      AI does not simply use fewer emotion words. It uses a narrower emotional palette.

      In 300 paired movie reviews, AI and humans write about the same amount of feeling — but not with the same vocabulary. Humans use 204 distinct emotion words across their reviews. AI uses 117, and leans on its favorites even harder: just 10 words cover over half of everything it writes.
